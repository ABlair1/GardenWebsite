Making Moves in Your Garden

The moment has finally arrived! Now you can get your hands dirty and set your garden in motion. Ideally, you've been eagerly planning and reading all winter and spring is right around the corner. If that is not the case, and it is already spring or summer as you're reading this, don't worry! You can still get ready for a late summer and early fall crop, which can vary depending on your location (more on that below). However, for our purposes, we will assume that you are going to plant your garden in the spring.

Soil and Nutrients

It's what plants crave! We will assume that you've picked a nice sunny spot where your plants can get all the solar energy they could ever want, and that you are watering them appropriately. Let's talk about the other things they will need to grow.

Nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium/potash (commonly referred to as N-P-K) are the primary nutrients that plants need to grow and produce. Most fertilizers focus on these big three to give plants much of the food they will need. There are also many trace nutrients and minerals that are vitally important to plants, such as calcium, magnesium, etc., but we will not go into that here. N, P, and K should be the first thing you learn about in regards to plant nutrition. Soil pH is equally as important! If soil is too acidic (low) or basic (high), then plants will not be able to absorb the nutrients that are in the soil. Many plants do well somewhere between 6.0 and 7.5 on the pH scale, but you should definitely research what is best for your chosen plants.

To determine the pH and nutrient levels of your soil, you have a couple of options. If you're serious about gardening, I would recommend having a credible soil lab test a sample of your garden soil before adding any fertilizer or ammendments. I am on the east coast of the U.S. and my preferred lab is at the University of Delaware. There is probably a university closer to you where you can have a test performed. Often this consists of sending a couple small bags of soil through the mail and getting a full report back some weeks later. You can also perform an at-home soil test using a kit (pictured below). These kits are not at all as accurate as a lab test, but they can be useful for giving you a very general snapshot of what is going on. Last year I sent my soil to lab, but this year I decided to try a test kit. Test kits can also be used throughout the growing season to measure how conditions change over time. Seen below, I have enlarged the images of my results from the test kit for clarity. Comparing the results to the provided scales, we can see that this sample was deficient in nitrogen, but the other values were acceptable. This lets us know how much fertilizer to use, and of what type.

When to Plant

Ideally, the best time to plant outdoors in when the last risk of frost has passed. This can vary widely by your geographic location. Assuming you live in the U.S., the U.S. Department of Agriculture provides a Plant Hardiness Zone Map. This resource lets users look up the average minimum temperature in their area. Often, hardiness zones will also correlate with last dates of frost. In this way, you can look up when to plant in your area. No matter where you live, you can get a jump on the season by starting seedlings indoors several weeks before the last frost.

Starting Seeds Indoors

If you're going to buy started plants, you can skip over the rest of this page and head to the next one. Otherwise, lets talk about seeds! Hopefully, you have seeds picked out from a supplier already and you're ready to rock! Now look at those seed packets, or online if they haven't arrived yet. Many seed seed packets will list recommendations for when and how to plant them. For example, one of my tomato varieties says to start indoors, 6 to 8 weeks before outdoor night temperatures are around 55 degrees F. Then the package goes on to recommend sowing seeds 0.25 inches deep and one inch apart, and provides further directions. Just do whatever your seed packets recommend! It is that simple!

When starting seeds indoors, you must provide them with a growing light specifically designed for plants. This will provide the strong light needed to start seeds and get them going on the right foot (or root). Many companies sell a soil mix specifically for starting seeds, and these work well, but you could also mix peat moss and perlite in a 2:1 ratio and your plants would be fine. Seeds come packaged naturaly with all the nutrients they will need to start life, right in their little seed pods. When they have grown a few sets of leaves, then you can transplant them and provide fertilizer.

Starting Seeds Outdoors

Some plant seeds do best when sown directly in the ground, and struggle when started inside and transplanted. Or maybe you just didn't get around to starting those seeds back in March or April, that is ok too! Follow the directions on the seed packets the same as we discussed above. Some packets will have different recommendations for starting outdoors. When the weather is warm and it is sunny, they will sprout before you know it!
