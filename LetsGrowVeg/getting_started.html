Getting Started with Your Garden

Welcome! If you're here, then you're interested in learning more about gardening and getting some green on your thumbs. Don't worry, it's a lot easier than you may think! The most important thing is having an interest and enjoying yourself. Let's jump in!

Garden Types

Home gardens can range in size and type from over an acre in crops, to herbs in a window box, and everything in between. No matter the space or resources you have available, there is almost always some sort of garden that will be a perfect fit for you.

Traditional Rows

When talking about traditional rows, I mean any planting that is done at ground level and in a space that is specially set aside only for growing vegetables. This is what you may think of when you picture a farm. While it may seem like a lot, row planting doesn't have to be intimidating. You do not have to plant a whole field of crops. Rows also don't have to be in big rectangular plots. You can plant a row anywhere there is space available. You could convert some flower beds, or create new rows in an unused space at the edge of your yard, for example. I'd recommend rows that are 2-3 feet wide and as long as you would like. The quality of soil and how it is managed can also affect how well this option could work for you.

Pros: Cons:
  • Living soil with worms, microbes, etc.
  • Nurtients in soil (healthy).
  • Access to groundwater.
  • Gets better every year (managed correctly).
  • Soil type may be poor or difficult to work.
  • Potentially nutrient deficienct soil (unhealthy).
  • Weeds grow and spread easily.
  • May take time to get up and running.

Containers

Containers offer many benefits and could be anything from terra cotta pots to plastic five gallon buckets, repurposed storage bins, or simple wooden boxes of any size. Containers are defined by having an enclosed bottom that is not in direct contact with the ground. They may or may not be on raised platforms or stands, depending on their size and location. Whatever your container, make sure there are holes in the bottom so that water can easily drain out and not drown plant roots. Personally, I love growing in fabric pots. They are made from a woven textile that allows air exchange with the soil in the pot and for excess water to drain freely without the need for holes in the bottom.

Pros: Cons:
  • Able to grow places where there is no ground (balcony, patio, etc.).
  • Fewer weeds and pests.
  • Control the nutrients that go into the soil.
  • Containers can be moved as needed.
  • Need to refresh or replace soil every year.
  • Over or under-watering more common.
  • Limited root space for larger plants.
  • No worms and fewer microbes (soil is not as "alive").

Raised Beds

Raised beds are the best of both worlds when compared to row planting and containers. They can be thought of as a large container of sorts, which has no bottom at all, and thus allows plants to freely interact with the living soil in the ground. They can offer greater control for a gardener than planting at ground level, and without the constraints of containers.

Pros: Cons:
  • Fewer weeds and pests.
  • Good drainage.
  • Living soil and root access to ground/groundwater.
  • Control over growing space and soil.
  • Filling/building a raised bed initially takes a lot soil.
  • May take time to get up and running.
  • Requires ground space (same as rows).

Deciding What to Grow

This is pretty straightforward. What do you want to grow? In order to answer that question, it's important to think about any potential limitations you may have. For example, if you try to grow a squash plant in a small pot on your patio, you will be very disappointed with the outcome. However, herbs such as oregano or thyme could thrive in such a container. I'd suggest figuring out what type of garden you want and how much space you're working with, then move on to deciding which plants you'll grow there.

Order a seed catalogue or go online to a reputable seed company's website. Browse the available options and see what conditions each plant needs to thrive. A good company will list plant spacing and size, ideal growing conditions, and give you an idea about what to expect. Then you will be free to either order seeds online or just make a list of plants you want. You could take this plant list to a local nursery to buy seedlings that have already been started for you (which makes life easy). Before that, however, continue reading on and figure out a plan for the big picture.

Side Note: My go-to company for seeds is Renee's Garden.

Choosing a Location

Do you know which locations around your property get the most sunlight throughout the day? If not, now is the time to find out! Most vegetable plants require 8 to 12 hours of sunlight daily. If you have a lot of trees on your proerty, or other buildings shade out much of your yard, this can be problematic. If your plants don't get enough light, they may not become very robust or produce much. My advice is to look around the property early in the morning one day and find the sunny spots. Then check back every few hours and take note of how the sunny spots change throughout the day, all the way until sunset. Then just choose the spots that get the most light and are in a workable location (you need to be able to access your garden after all).

Tools and Equipment

  • Hose and/or watering can
    • (I recommend a watering wand with on/off valve for your hose)
  • Garden fork
  • Long-handled shovel for digging
  • Hand shovel for planting
  • Dirt rake (hard rake)
  • Thermometer
  • Simple rain gauge
  • Garden stakes
  • Tomato cages and/or trellis (as needed)
  • Containers or materials to build/prep growing space (as needed)
  • Compost and soil (obviously)
  • Other soil amendments and fertilizer as needed
  • Scissors for harvesting produce
